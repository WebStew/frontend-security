Front-end Security Part 1 : Introduction

Introduction

Welcome to my first portal blog post. The plan for the first series of articles is to better equip us front-end developers on what types of application attacks we should be aware of and what techniques and resources are available, recommended and implemented by the community to combat against them. At the moment I am not sure how many parts this series will entail but I'll endeavour to keep it structured and point readers to helpful content around the internet relating to specific vulnerabilities.

So what is security?

Well Google describes

security
as the state of being free from danger or threat

This definition seems a very black and white description - can you ever be completely free from danger or threat? Perhaps a better definition is actions taken to achieve a state which mitigates against threat or danger?

So what are the threats?

Threats or system vulnerabilities within technology are generally referred to as attack vectors. An

attack vector
is a path or means by which a hacker can gain access to a computer or network server in order to deliver a payload or malicious outcome. Attack vectors enable hackers to exploit system vulnerabilities, including the human element.
There is an extensive list of these attack vectors within web applications that we should be aware of as front-end developers. Knowing about these different attach vectors will be our best attribute enabling us to take the appropriate action for mitigating against them.

So it is said that if you know your enemies and know yourself, you will not be put at risk even in a hundred battles. The Art of War

Resources

In my opinion some of the best resources available on the internet to help harden your application security is the Open Web Application Security Project ( OWASP ) and I will frequently refer readers to their content, suggested solutions and security hole pluggers within this series. There is a wealth of information gathered within this project which is useful to both back and front end developers, software testers and users of web applications.

The OWASP core purpose being :

Be the thriving global community that drives visibility and evolution in the safety and security of the world's software.
